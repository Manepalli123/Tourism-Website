The Great Wall of China

Visit Here
Early Walls: earliest walls and fortifications in China were built as early as the 7th century BCE. These were constructed by individual states to defend against invasions from rival states. The walls were made of materials such as earth, wood, and stones.
Qin Dynasty: The construction of the Great Wall on a larger scale began during the Qin Dynasty (221-206 BCE) under the rule of Emperor Qin Shi Huang. To unify the country and protect it from northern nomadic tribes, Emperor Qin connected and extended existing walls. The labor force for the construction consisted of soldiers, peasants, and prisoners. The wall was initially made of rammed earth and later reinforced with stone.
Han Dynasty:During the Han Dynasty (206 BCE-220 CE), the Great Wall underwent further extensions and renovations. The purpose remained primarily defensive, and the wall was extended westward to protect the Silk Road trade routes.
Ming Dynasty:The most well-known and well-preserved sections of the Great Wall were built during the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644 CE). The construction was carried out on a massive scale, using bricks and stone blocks. The Ming Dynasty's walls were designed to withstand attacks from cannons and firearms. During this period, the wall was extended, repaired, and fortified to protect against Mongol invasions.
Purpose and Function:The primary purpose of the Great Wall was defense. It acted as a physical barrier to protect China's agricultural lands and cities from raids and invasions by various nomadic tribes from the north, such as the Mongols and Xiongnu. It also served as a means of communication and transportation, with signal towers and beacon fires used to relay messages.

Petra is an ancient city that lies in present-day Jordan and dates back to the fourth century B.C. Ruins of the once-great metropolis and trading center now serve as an important archeologic site and tourist attraction.
The name Petra originates from the Greek word 'petros' which means rocks. Petra is also known as Al-Batra in Arabic. Between 1BC and 8 AD, Petra experienced severe earthquakes and in 362 AD half of the city was destroyed.
Petra is the spot where Moses struck a rock with his staff and water came forth, and where Moses' brother, Aaron, is buried, at Mount Hor, known today as Jabal Haroun or Mount Aaron.

Machu Picchu

Visit Here
Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Inca citadel located in the Eastern Cordillera of southern Peru on a 2,430-meter (7,970 ft) mountain ridge. Often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas", it is the most familiar icon of the Inca Empire.
More than 7,000 feet above sea level in the Andes Mountains, Machu Picchu is the most visited tourist destination in Peru. A symbol of the Incan Empire and built around 1450AD, Machu Picchu was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007.
Historians say explorers got it wrong: It's not Machu Picchu, it's Huayna Picchu. The Inca citadel known as Machu Picchu is pictured with the Huayna Picchu mountain in the background. Peru's Machu Picchu is more than 600 years old and yet, experts continue to learn new things about the iconic archaeological site.

Christ the Redeemer

Visit Here
Christ the Redeemer, a colossal statue of Jesus Christ, holds a prominent position in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and sculpted by French artist Paul Landowski, construction of the statue began in 1922 and concluded in 1931. Perched atop Corcovado mountain in the Tijuca Forest National Park, the imposing figure of Christ stands at a towering height of approximately 98 feet (30 meters) with its arms extending over 92 feet (28 meters) wide. Embodying profound religious significance, Christ the Redeemer is an iconic Christian symbol, portraying Jesus Christ with outstretched arms, symbolizing peace and welcome to the world. Its representation of the Savior has made it a popular destination for religious pilgrims and tourists from across the globe. In 2007, the statue, together with the picturesque Rio de Janeiro landscape, was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, acknowledging its cultural and historical importance. To reach the statue, visitors can opt for a train or van ride that ascends the scenic Corcovado mountain. Once at the summit, they are rewarded with breathtaking panoramic views of Rio de Janeiro and its surroundings. By night, Christ the Redeemer comes to life as it is beautifully illuminated, creating a captivating sight visible from various vantage points in the city. Throughout the years, the statue has undergone several restoration and maintenance efforts to ensure its structural integrity and preserve its iconic appearance. A symbol of Brazil's hospitality and faith, Christ the Redeemer continues to attract millions of visitors, leaving them in awe of its magnificence and the remarkable view it offers of Rio de Janeiro.

Colosseum

Visit Here
The Colosseum in Rome, Italy, is a large amphitheater that hosted events like gladiatorial games. Design Pics Inc. The Colosseum, also named the Flavian Amphitheater, is a large amphitheater in Rome. It was built during the reign of the Flavian emperors as a gift to the Roman people.
The Colosseum was built as part of an imperial effort to revitalize Rome after the tumultuous year of the four emperors, 69 CE. As with other amphitheatres, the emperor Vespasian intended the Colosseum to be an entertainment venue, hosting gladiator fights, animal hunts, and even mock naval battles.
Severe damage was inflicted on the Colosseum by the great earthquake in 1349, causing the outer south side, lying on a less stable alluvial terrain, to collapse. Much of the tumbled stone was reused to build palaces, churches, hospitals and other buildings elsewhere in Rome.

Taj Mahal

Visit Here
The Taj Mahal is a mausoleum complex in Agra, western Uttar Pradesh state, northern India. It is considered the finest example of Mughal architecture (a blend of Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles). The Taj Mahal is also one of the world's most iconic monuments, visited by millions of tourists each year. The complex was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1983.
It was built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal with construction starting in 1632 AD and completed in 1648 AD, with the mosque, the guest house and the main gateway on the south, the outer courtyard and its cloisters were added subsequently and completed in 1653 AD.
The Taj Mahal is a mausoleum complex that houses the tombs of Mumtaz Mahal (“Chosen One of the Palace”) and her husband, the Mughal emperor Shah Jahān (reigned 1628–58).
